Environmental Impact

Organic waste is biodegradable, but when sent to landfills, it decomposes without oxygen (anaerobically), producing methaneâ€”a greenhouse gas 25 times more potent than carbon dioxide. Additionally, nutrients that could enrich soil are wasted. Food waste specifically represents a significant loss of resources including water, energy, land, and labor used in food production, processing, and transportation.
Methane emissions: organic waste in landfills is a major source of this potent greenhouse gas
Resource waste: discarded food represents wasted water, energy, and agricultural inputs
Leachate production: decomposing organics in landfills create liquid that can contaminate groundwater
Lost opportunity: valuable nutrients that could rebuild soil health are discarded

What can we do?

We can compost organic waste at home or through community programs, plan meals carefully to reduce food waste, and store food properly to extend its shelf life. Learning to use food scraps creatively in cooking also minimizes waste.

Home composting turns kitchen scraps and yard waste into nutrient-rich soil for gardens. Many food scraps like citrus peels can be used to make natural cleaning products, while coffee grounds serve as garden fertilizer or natural deodorizers. By diverting organic waste from landfills, we reduce methane emissions and complete the natural nutrient cycle.
